At-a-Glance View

This page pulls together the weekly learning goals, family checklist items, and quick conversation starters so you can see the shape of the week at a glance.

Week 1 · Apr 7–10

Literacy skills: Understanding what makes poetry poetry, using sensory details, noticing theme, and trying blackout poetry.

Theme skills: Building curiosity about hidden histories and comparing “then and now.”

Ask at home:

  • What topic are you starting to shine a light on?
  • What hidden history surprised you most?
  • What detail helped you think like a poet or historian?

Week 2 · Apr 13–16

Literacy skills: Lyric poems, rhyme, repetition, and early peer feedback.

Theme skills: Exploration technologies, trade, colonization, and supported opinions.

Ask at home:

  • What theme are you trying to show in your lyric poem?
  • What did you learn that felt important or complicated?
  • What feedback changed your thinking?

Week 3 · Apr 20–23

Literacy skills: Symbolism, free verse, and revision with feedback partners.

Theme skills: Distinguishing primary and secondary sources and using evidence.

Ask at home:

  • What symbol did you choose in your poem?
  • What hidden history are you researching?
  • What evidence helped you understand your topic better?

Week 4 · Apr 27–30

Literacy skills: Narrative poems, plot, voice, pacing, and detail.

Theme skills: Choosing how to tell a hidden-history story and practicing respectful disagreement.

Ask at home:

  • What story is happening in your narrative poem?
  • Which project format did you choose, and why?
  • What are you excited to build or create next?

Week 5 · May 4–7

Literacy skills: Golden shovel poems and final polishing.

Theme skills: Outlining projects, refining claims, and preparing presentations.

Ask at home:

  • What line or word choice are you most proud of?
  • What is your claim in theme workshop?
  • What feedback helped you improve your work?

Week 6 · May 11–14

Literacy skills: Reflecting on growth and sharing favorite poems.

Theme skills: Final discussion, rehearsal, Expo prep, and presenting to an audience.

Ask at home:

  • Which poem or part of your writing growth are you most excited to share?
  • What big idea do you want people to understand from your project?
  • How have you grown this cycle?
