At-a-Glance View

This page pulls together the weekly learning goals, family checklist items, and conversation starters so you can see the shape of the cycle at a glance.

Week 1 · Apr 7–10Foundations
Literacy skills
Understanding what makes poetry poetry, using sensory details, noticing theme, and trying blackout poetry as a low-stakes entry point.
Theme skills
Building curiosity about hidden histories, comparing “then and now,” and beginning to ask stronger questions about how everyday objects connect to the past.
Family checklist + weekly blurb
□ Learners will work daily in project journal
□ Learners will work daily in literacy journal
□ Daily Math Practice
□ Be sure to keep up with reading schedule
□ Have learner look at possible feedback and revise when needed

This week is all about launching curiosity. Learners are trying on the roles of poet and historian, so journal work may include brainstorming, noticing details, and making early reflections.

Ask:
1. What topic or idea are you starting to shine a light on in your literacy journal?
2. What object or hidden history surprised you most in your project journal this week?
3. What is one detail you noticed that helped you think like a poet or historian?
Week 2 · Apr 13–16Claims & craft
Literacy skills
Analyzing lyric poems, experimenting with rhyme and repetition, and giving early peer feedback to strengthen word choice and big ideas.
Theme skills
Learning about exploration technologies, trade, colonization, and practicing how to make and support an opinion during discussion.
Family checklist + weekly blurb
□ Learners will work daily in project journal
□ Learners will work daily in literacy journal
□ Daily Math Practice
□ Be sure to keep up with reading schedule
□ Have learner look at possible feedback and revise when needed

This week learners begin shaping stronger claims. Their journal work may show early poem planning, summaries of cause and effect, and opinions supported with reasons.

Ask:
1. What theme are you trying to show in your lyric poem?
2. What did you learn about trade, colonization, or technology that felt important or complicated?
3. What feedback did you get this week, and how did it change your thinking or your writing?
Week 3 · Apr 20–23Evidence
Literacy skills
Exploring symbolism and free verse, reading closely for message, and revising poems with help from feedback partners.
Theme skills
Becoming “history detectives,” distinguishing primary and secondary sources, and using evidence to support claims about hidden histories.
Family checklist + weekly blurb
□ Learners will work daily in project journal
□ Learners will work daily in literacy journal
□ Daily Math Practice
□ Be sure to keep up with reading schedule
□ Have learner look at possible feedback and revise when needed

This week is detective mode. Learners are digging for symbols, evidence, and research details, so their journal pages may include notes, sources, and connections across ideas.

Ask:
1. What symbol did you choose in your free verse poem, and what deeper meaning does it carry?
2. What hidden history are you researching, and why does it matter?
3. What evidence or fact from your project journal helped you understand your topic better?
Week 4 · Apr 27–30Storytelling
Literacy skills
Studying narrative poems, creating poems with plot and voice, and improving sequence, pacing, and detail through revision.
Theme skills
Choosing how to tell a hidden-history story, researching supportive sources, and practicing respectful disagreement in discussion.
Family checklist + weekly blurb
□ Learners will work daily in project journal
□ Learners will work daily in literacy journal
□ Daily Math Practice
□ Be sure to keep up with reading schedule
□ Have learner look at possible feedback and revise when needed

This week learners become storytellers and makers. Journals may show narrative planning, project sketches, material choices, and explanations for creative decisions.

Ask:
1. What story is happening in your narrative poem?
2. Which project format did you choose for theme workshop, and why does it fit your hidden history?
3. What did you plan or sketch this week that you are excited to build or create next?
Week 5 · May 4–7Polish
Literacy skills
Writing golden shovel poems, making thoughtful craft choices, and using peer feedback to polish final pieces.
Theme skills
Outlining projects, refining claims, giving and receiving feedback, and preparing presentations that combine research with clear communication.
Family checklist + weekly blurb
□ Learners will work daily in project journal
□ Learners will work daily in literacy journal
□ Daily Math Practice
□ Be sure to keep up with reading schedule
□ Have learner look at possible feedback and revise when needed

This week is revision and polish time. Learners are using feedback to make their work clearer, stronger, and more ready for sharing.

Ask:
1. What line, image, or word choice are you most proud of in your poetry this week?
2. What is your claim in theme workshop, and how are you making it stronger?
3. What feedback helped you improve your project or poem before the final stretch?
Week 6 · May 11–14Celebrate & share
Literacy skills
Reflecting on growth as writers, sharing favorite poems aloud during cohort campfire days, and celebrating voice, revision, and risk-taking.
Theme skills
Final discussion, presentation rehearsal, Expo prep, and confidently sharing a polished hidden-history project with an audience.
Family checklist + weekly blurb
□ Learners will work daily in project journal
□ Learners will work daily in literacy journal
□ Daily Math Practice
□ Be sure to keep up with reading schedule
□ Have learner look at possible feedback and revise when needed

This week is about reflection, rehearsal, and celebration. Learners will be wrapping up, sharing aloud, and getting ready to present their thinking with confidence.

Ask:
1. Which poem or part of your writing growth are you most excited to share at campfire?
2. What is one big idea you want people to understand from your hidden-history project?
3. How have you grown this cycle as a writer, researcher, or presenter?
